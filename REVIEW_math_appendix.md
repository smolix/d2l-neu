## 1. Executive summary

**The appendix is already at or near the stated bar, and in several places past it.** All six chapters come out at A−/A against the strongest available comparisons (MML, Goodfellow Part I, Boyd & Vandenberghe, MIT 18.063/18.S096, Särkkä–Solin, the Flow Matching Guide, MIT 6.S184, Polyanskiy–Wu). Three sections are plausibly the best treatments of their material that exist at textbook level in 2026: **§23.3 Matrix Calculus & Autodiff** (two working AD engines — dual numbers and a tape — verified against all four frameworks, plus the associativity ⇒ forward/reverse framing), **§27.3 Fokker–Planck & Probability Flow** (the λ-family proposition deriving Anderson's theorem and the probability-flow ODE simultaneously, explaining the factor of two), and **§26.3's estimation-hardness treatment** (the log N ceiling stated, simulated with a perfect critic, and met again by Fano's floor — in no textbook anywhere).

**Correctness is remarkably high.** Across ~33,000 lines, the reviewers recomputed hundreds of constants, rates, densities, KL values, KKT residuals, and convergence orders. The harvest of genuine errors is small: **one real mathematical error in prose** (cross-entropy claimed reparameterization-invariant, §26.1 — it is not; only KL is), one wrong figure caption (§26.3's NWJ "low-variance" claim contradicted by the chapter's own experiment), two prose/output mismatches (ch. 24's "three of the six coordinates" is two; ch. 27's "six decades" is nine), one factor-of-2 slip (ch. 27 "±8" is ±4), one stale cross-reference (§23.3 promises a derivation §23.2 no longer contains), and **a cluster of slide-only errors** (Cauchy variance "diverges like log R"; Cauchy/Student-t exponential-family exclusion "because their support moves"). Everything else the agents checked — including the subtle claims (power-iteration squared rate, Gavish–Donoho constant, IB critical β, DDPM √(1−β) vs 1−β/2, Pinsker sharpness, Welford exactness) — is correct.

**The differentiator is the verification loop, and it is executed consistently:** essentially every proposition is followed by a cell that measures it, and the executed outputs match the prose *quantitatively* (all four framework stores are clean; one visible `UserWarning` in §25.2's pytorch exp-family cell is the only blemish). This is the thing no competitor does — MML/Strang aren't notebook-native, VMLS/Bach stop short of DL, the 2024–26 diffusion material is fragmented — and the research pass confirms **nobody currently combines executable notebooks + foundations + 2026 generative/LLM-relevant math**. The niche is real and this appendix is positioned to own it.

**What remains is targeted, not structural:** (a) the confirmed-error fix list (§3, an afternoon of work); (b) ~a dozen high-value code cells where the prose asserts something the notebook never runs (Muon's Newton–Schulz, forward-vs-reverse cost, a CLT overlay, a reflow round, an fp16 loss-scaling step, edge-of-stability); (c) ~15 figures for passages that paint pictures in words (Lagrange tangency, water-filling basins, defective shear, LoRA schematic, stability regions, checkpointing, DPI pipes, Tweedie); (d) one substantial coverage hole — **the mathematics of adaptive optimizers (AdaGrad/Adam/AdamW) is one sentence in a 5,800-line optimization chapter** — plus a small set of section-level additions (implicit function theorem, concentration beyond Chebyshev, arithmetic coding/"an LM is a compressor", the score↔velocity dictionary); and (e) an appendix-level decision on 2–4 new topics (§4).

## 2. Cross-cutting assessment

**Teaching clarity & structure.** House style (3–5 top-level `##` sections, intuition-first Proposition/Proof, Summary + Exercises) holds across all 23 sections with only two structural nits (§23.2's two-paragraph "Bridge" stub; §23.4's Fubini filed under Change of Variables). Narrative threading is a real strength — promissory notes are systematically issued and paid off (defective shear → SVD; small-change identity through all of ch. 23; the κ thread through ch. 24; "one scaling law" through ch. 27.2; the regression lemma reused verbatim for DSM and CFM). Proof hygiene is unusually good: hypotheses are stated (identifiability, constraint qualification, absolute integrability), and honesty flags ("sampling can only refute", the heavy-ball counterexample, MI-estimates-as-bounds, the compression-phase debate) appear exactly where experts would want them.

**Code.** Of ~120 code cells inventoried, effectively **zero are drawing-only plumbing** — the post-refactor rule has been enforced. The only borderline mass is per-framework duplication of pure-plot cells (`linear-approximation`, `taylor-series` in §23.1 could be untagged pure Python) and the 4×~25-line Fashion-MNIST loading cell in §22.1. The recurring *deficit* is the mirror image: several sections' **central quantitative claims never touch silicon** — see the code-additions list in §3.

**Figures.** 161 figure includes across the six chapters, captions consistently substantive (several captions — the ELBO/EM caption in §25.3, the KKT cone in §24.3 — are miniature proofs). Density is uneven: ch. 23 (51) and ch. 22 (33) are well-served; **§26.1 has two figures in ~1,150 lines while two finished on-topic SVGs (`mdl-it-code-length-bars`, `mdl-it-kraft-tree`) sit in `img/` used only by slides** — promote them; §24.2 has 3 figures per 1,491 lines. One orphan asset (`img/mdl-it-mutual-information.svg`, referenced nowhere). The ~15 concrete new-figure suggestions in the chapter reviews are all passages that currently paint a picture in words.

**Slides vs prose (systemic).** The two worst factual errors found anywhere are **slide-only** (Cauchy log R; exponential-family support claim), and the survey of ch. 2–5 found the complementary failure (derivations that exist *only* in slides: Markov's inequality, the manual regression gradient, the spectral-radius argument). Slides are out of scope for this review, but the pattern warrants a dedicated slide-vs-prose QA pass when slides are revisited: slides compress prose nuance into false statements, and prose sometimes delegates rigor to slides.

**Mechanical consistency debt** (sweep once): tab-style mix in ch. 22 (`%%tab` + `load_ext` in svd-low-rank vs `#@tab` elsewhere); Discussions blocks inconsistent across files (real topic ids / placeholder `/t/divergences` / bare root URLs / pytorch-only tabs / §27.3's substantive prose paragraph under the Discussions heading); figure-label naming drift in ch. 25 (`fig_mdl-prob-*` vs `fig_mdl-sampling-distribution`); four inline citations in §24.3 not `:cite:`-keyed; a stray H1 inside §24.4's slides block (`mdl-numerical-stability-conditioning.md:1039`); `@` missing `!` at `mdl-gradient-based-optimization.md:1318`; and the repo `CLAUDE.md` "Current status" note still calling ch. 24/26/27 "detailed ToCs + reading lists" (all three are fully written — stale).

## 3. Priority fix list

### Confirmed errors (all re-verified against source; fix before anything else)

1. **§26.1 `mdl-information-theory.md:175–178`** — "cross-entropy and KL divergence, which *are* invariant under reparameterization" is **false for cross-entropy**: CE = H(P) + KL picks up the E_P[log|det J|] term exactly like differential entropy; the Jacobians cancel only inside the KL ratio. The one genuine math error in the appendix prose. Claim invariance for KL only.
2. **§26.3 `mdl-mutual-information.md:783`** (figure caption) — "NWJ / Barber–Agakov is low-variance but biased downward … DV/MINE is nearly unbiased but spreads" contradicts the section's own experiment (NWJ = 1.23 **± 11.74** at true MI 6) and Poole et al.'s taxonomy. Rewrite caption (BA: low-variance/high-bias; NWJ: high-variance) and re-check the figure against it.
3. **§24.3 `mdl-constrained-optimization-duality.md:497`** (and slide `:1239`) — "three of the six coordinates came back exactly zero": the committed output is `[0.0676, 0., 0.5823, 0.0467, 0., 0.3034]` — **two** zeros.
4. **§27.1 `mdl-odes-solvers.md:619–621`** — "RK4 sits *six decades* below Euler": 3.6e-3 vs 5.0e-12 is ~**nine** decades (the very next clause, "a million times the accuracy," is also understated).
5. **§27.4 `mdl-score-matching-diffusion-flow.md:347–349`** — score curve "values span ±8": the σ-smoothed mixture (means ±2, component variance 0.5) spans **±4**; likely computed with the clean variance 0.25.
6. **§23.3 `mdl-matrix-calculus-autodiff.md:336–339`** — the factorization gradient ∂_V‖X−UV‖² "derived in :numref:`sec_mdl-multivariable_calculus`" — the rebuilt §23.2 contains no such derivation. Stale legacy pointer: derive it in place (3 lines in the section's own style) or drop the clause.
7. **§25.1 slide `mdl-random-variables.md:1245`** — Cauchy variance "diverges like log R": it diverges **linearly** ((2/π)(R − arctan R)); the cell's own outputs (5.4 → 63 → 636 per decade of R) contradict the slide. (The *mean's* absolute integral is the log R one; prose is correct.)
8. **§25.2 slide `mdl-distributions.md:1577`** — Cauchy/Student-t excluded from the exponential family "because their support moves": their support is fixed ℝ; only the uniforms are excluded by support. The prose (:979–987) gives the correct reason (no finite-dim sufficient statistic).
9. **§22.1 `mdl-geometry-linear-algebraic-ops.md:1161,1394` vs `:1017,1092`** — **B is silently redefined as its transpose** mid-section ([[2,−1],[4,−2]] → [[2,4],[−1,−2]]). Each local claim is correct but a careful reader computing along will get "wrong" columns. Use one matrix throughout.
10. **§25.2 `#distributions-exp-family` (pytorch)** — stored output ships a visible `UserWarning` (requires_grad tensor → scalar). Add `.detach()`, re-capture.
11. **§24.1 `:529–536` vs §24.2 `:634–642`** — the strongly-convex GD theorem promised in §24.1 (iterate-distance form ‖x_k−x*‖²) is not the statement §24.2 proves (function-value form). Both true, but the promised proof never discharges the stated inequality. Align the statements.
12. **§27.2 `mdl-sdes.md:855–857`** — "the leftover half-percent is the EM discretization bias … *not statistics*": EM bias explains +0.001 of the +0.0045 gap; the MC standard error is ≈0.006. The gap **is** mostly statistics — the sentence asserts the opposite.
13. **§24.3 `:792–793`** — "far inside the 10⁻⁴ we asked for": the cell has no tolerance (fixed 5000 iterations). Dangling reference.
14. **§27.2 slide `:1064–1065`** — "a deterministic flow … cannot smoothly turn data into pure Gaussian noise" is falsified by §27.4's flow matching. The correct claim is about a *fixed, data-independent* flow sending every data law to the same Gaussian. Sharpen slide and the related prose at :80–88.

Minor issues (correct-but-imprecise wording, missing hypotheses, citation nits, dead variables, degenerate example points) are catalogued per-section in §5; the highest-value ones: the degenerate backprop evaluation point in §23.2 (b = 0 makes all four printed gradients identical at −4096 — pick a non-degenerate point), Weyl's inequality missing "symmetric perturbation" (§22.2:650), the PL box missing L-smoothness (§24.2:891), Gibbs' proof's silent supp(Q) assumption (§26.1:378), and the InfoNCE `L_NCE` definition that algebraically equals plain CE, making the "log N bookkeeping" remark a no-op (§26.3:796).

## 4. Appendix-level coverage: what to add, what to cut

### Add — inside existing chapters (highest priority first)

1. **Mathematics of adaptive methods (ch. 24).** AdaGrad's diagonal-preconditioner/regret derivation, Adam's bias correction, the Reddi et al. non-convergence example, AdamW's decoupling — currently one sentence in the Newton coda. This is the single largest gap in the appendix measured against 2026 practice (AdamW is *the* default optimizer). One ~1-page subsection + one ill-conditioned-quadratic cell. Add the nonconvex **SGD** stationarity rate (Ghadimi–Lan O(1/√K)) — §24.1 proves the deterministic version and calls it "the honest theorem about training networks," but training uses SGD. Name K-FAC/Shampoo/Muon in the Newton coda (Muon is already treated in ch. 22 — cross-link). Give warmup more than two words.
2. **Concentration beyond Chebyshev + high-dimensional phenomena (ch. 25).** Hoeffding/sub-Gaussian with a Chernoff-sketch proof and a Chebyshev-vs-true-tail simulation; plus a short "probability in high dimension" pass under the MVN (‖x‖ ≈ √d, near-orthogonality — reuse §22.1's cosine-concentration result, cross-linked). This also repairs a **hand-off break**: ch. 4 states and *uses* Hoeffding (`generalization-classification.md:199–227`) and nothing anywhere proves or even restates it.
3. **Implicit function theorem / differentiating through a solver (ch. 23).** The only item in the standard DL-calculus canon with zero presence; underpins the adjoint method ch. 27 uses, DEQs, and bilevel optimization. One subsection or a meaty verified exercise in §23.3.
4. **Arithmetic coding & "language modeling is compression" (ch. 26).** The chapter proves KL = extra bits for symbol codes but stops one step short of the modern punchline (loss × token count = compressed size; Delétang et al. 2023). Add entropy rate (invoked at §26.1:869 but never defined) and one paragraph on cross-entropy **scaling laws** in the perplexity subsection — the defining empirical fact about the chapter's central quantity, currently unmentioned. MDL/two-part codes as a further-reading paragraph.
5. **Score↔velocity↔ε↔v dictionary (ch. 27).** One display (for Gaussian paths, u_t in terms of ∇log p_t) makes "flow matching subsumes diffusion" an identity instead of a slogan, closes the unifying table algebraically, and is where v-prediction (what production models actually train) belongs. Table-stakes in the Flow Matching Guide and 6.S184.
6. **Optimizer-as-dynamics bridge (ch. 27 ↔ 24).** GD = forward Euler on gradient flow; the h < 2/λ stability bound *is* the learning-rate bound; momentum = discretized heavy-ball ODE. Half a page + one cell in §27.1; the chapter's thesis currently skips its oldest instance. Symmetric hooks: §24.1's noise-ball discussion should link to ch. 27's SDE view; §24.4 should link to the main book's `sec_numerical_stability`.
7. **fp8 + summation error/Kahan + stochastic rounding (ch. 24.4).** By 2026 fp8 is mainstream; the "three formats, three bargains" table wants its fourth row. The Welford build-dependence anecdote *is* a summation-order story and the concept is never named.
8. **Saddle-point/minimax view of duality (ch. 24.3)** — one proposition away from what's proved; unlocks the GAN/adversarial cross-reference. Plus proximal operators (one subsection bridging conjugates and projections).
9. Smaller: rank–nullity + subspace projection matrices + QR (ch. 22); calibration/reliability diagram (ch. 25.4 names miscalibration, demonstrates nothing — a stated goal of the chapter); Rényi/α-divergences one paragraph and proper scoring rules half-page (ch. 26); Leibniz rule with moving limits + naming the Gamma function (ch. 23.4); log-SNR clock/schedule invariance + likelihood weighting λ(t)=g² (ch. 27.4).

## 4a. Placement plan: where each addition goes, and how big

The guiding principle: only two gaps are big enough to justify new section files; everything else fits as a `###` subsection, a paragraph, a cell, or an exercise inside existing sections — which keeps the house structure (3–5 `##` per section) intact, since most target files are already at capacity.

### Two new sections (new `.md` files)

**1. `chapter_mdl-optimization/mdl-adaptive-stochastic-methods.md` — "Adaptive Methods and the Dynamics of Training" (~900–1,100 lines, sized like its siblings).**
ToC position: between `mdl-gradient-based-optimization` and `mdl-convexity` — it consumes §24.1's SGD machinery directly. Contents: the nonconvex SGD stationarity rate (Ghadimi–Lan) as the opening proposition; AdaGrad derived from the per-coordinate/preconditioner view; RMSProp → Adam with bias correction *derived*; the Reddi et al. non-convergence counterexample as a Proposition plus a ~6-line cell; AdamW decoupling (cross-ref §25.3's MAP/weight-decay); a real treatment of schedules — the c > 1/(2μ) trap for 1/k, warmup, cosine/WSD (the reference research found *no* canonical treatment of schedule math anywhere: a gap to own); closing with the preconditioning ladder Adam-diagonal → K-FAC/Shampoo → Muon (cross-ref the ch. 22 Muon material). Cells: Adam-vs-SGD on an ill-conditioned quadratic, the Reddi example, noise ball with/without decay. 2–3 figures. Do *not* grow §24.1 instead: it is already 1,413 lines with six `##` sections; a one-page subsection there would under-serve the topic given AdamW's centrality.

**2. `chapter_mdl-probability-statistics/mdl-concentration-generalization.md` — "Concentration and Generalization" (~1,100–1,300 lines).**
ToC position: after `mdl-statistics`, with naive Bayes moved behind it as the chapter capstone (final order: random variables → distributions → MLE → statistics → concentration & generalization → naive Bayes). This one file repairs three problems at once: the Hoeffding debt (ch. 4 states and uses it; nothing proves it), the broken Rademacher promise in `generalization-deep.md:169`, and the triple-telling of double descent. Arc: MGF/Chernoff method → Hoeffding with a house-style short proof → sub-Gaussian variables → a Chebyshev-vs-Hoeffding-vs-true-tail simulation → **high-dimensional phenomena** as its own `###` (‖x‖ ≈ √d, near-orthogonality — cross-ref ch. 22's cosine-concentration result rather than re-deriving) → union bound → uniform convergence for a finite class (the honest generalization bound, fully proved) → Rademacher complexity defined + computed for one linear class → "where the classical story fails": interpolation, double descent (this becomes the *single* full treatment; ch. 3/5 keep pointers), benign-overfitting remark. Best cell in it: a random-features regression sweep that *produces* the double-descent curve (CPU-cheap). Then re-point `generalization-deep.md:169` here.

### Chapter verdict

This is a very strong chapter — comfortably above Deisenroth/MML and the old d2l appendix, and within reach of the stated bar (Blitzstein-level pedagogy with Wasserman-level inference content, DL-connected throughout). The mathematics is essentially error-free in the prose: I recomputed every density, moment, Fisher information, power calculation, tail probability, and worked example, and they all check out; the executed outputs (pytorch store) confirm every numerical claim the prose makes (0.7725 vs exact 0.7731; CR floor 0.00105 vs empirical 0.001039; 84.27% MNIST accuracy; permutation p = 0.0197; n−1 correction 3.997 vs 2.665; etc.). ## 7. Reference landscape and calibration (2026)

*Web-research pass: what best-in-class looks like now, the topic checklist per chapter, topics outside the six chapters, and pedagogical devices worth adopting. Used as the calibration basis for §4.*

### Reference landscape

**Foundations (linear algebra / calculus / general math-for-ML)**

- **Deisenroth, Faisal & Ong, *Mathematics for Machine Learning*** (CUP 2020, free: https://mml-book.github.io/book/mml-book.pdf). Part I covers LA (Ch.2), matrix decompositions (Ch.4), vector calculus (Ch.5), probability (Ch.6). Distinctive: every math tool is built toward one of four worked ML endpoints (linear regression, PCA, GMM, SVM), so nothing is taught "just because"; ships companion Jupyter notebooks. The most direct competitor to this appendix.
- **Goodfellow, Bengio & Courville, *Deep Learning* Part I** (https://www.deeplearningbook.org/). Ch.2 is a terse DL-scoped LA review; Ch.4 (Numerical Computation) is the standout — overflow/underflow, conditioning, gradient methods, KKT — i.e. *calculus meets floating point*, rarely covered elsewhere.
- **Boyd & Vandenberghe, *VMLS*** (https://web.stanford.edu/~boyd/vmls/). Applied, near-proof-free LA/least-squares with Julia+Python companions; every concept immediately paired with a numeric example. Closest in spirit to d2l's executable-notebook philosophy.
- **Strang, 18.065 *Linear Algebra and Learning from Data*** (https://math.mit.edu/~gs/learningfromdata/, OCW + https://github.com/mitmath/18065). Signature device: the **A = CR** rank factorization as a unifying spine (rank → four subspaces → SVD → pseudo-inverse → optimal low-rank `A_k`), carried into a "construction of deep networks" part.
- **Edelman & Johnson (with Bright), *Matrix Calculus for Machine Learning and Beyond*** (MIT 18.063/18.S096; arXiv:2501.14787, https://github.com/mitmath/matrixcalc). Matrices as holistic objects under differentiation (no index-chasing); derivatives of factorizations; forward vs. adjoint/reverse mode motivated by FLOP counting. The most rigorous, most current (2025) matrix-calculus-for-ML treatment.
- **Parr & Howard, "The Matrix Calculus You Need for Deep Learning"** (arXiv:1802.01528, interactive: https://explained.ai/matrix-calculus/). Best accessible on-ramp; its explicit three-way chain-rule taxonomy (scalar / total-derivative / vector) prevents the classic student errors on shared-subexpression graphs.
- **Blondel & Roulet, *The Elements of Differentiable Programming*** (arXiv:2403.14606, v3 June 2025, ~450 pp). The modern reference for JVP/VJP, autodiff through control flow, and "designing programs for differentiation," framed via twin optimization + probability lenses.
- **3Blue1Brown, *Essence of Linear Algebra* / *Essence of Calculus*** (https://www.3blue1brown.com/). Purely geometric, animation-driven intuition-before-symbols; the strongest model for "picture first, algebra second."

**Optimization / learning theory**

- **Boyd & Vandenberghe, *Convex Optimization*** (free: https://web.stanford.edu/~boyd/cvxbook/bv_cvxbook.pdf; EE364A still teaches it unchanged in 2026). Gold standard for geometric intuition — every abstraction (separating hyperplanes, duality gap) gets a picture before algebra.
- **Bach, *Learning Theory from First Principles*** (MIT Press 2024, free draft: https://www.di.ens.fr/~fbach/ltfp_book.pdf). Derives every convergence rate and generalization bound from scratch, with runnable code reproducing *every figure* — the closest existing text to d2l's "code teaches" rule.
- **Roberts, Yaida & Hanin, *The Principles of Deep Learning Theory*** (arXiv:2106.10165, https://deeplearningtheory.com/). Physics-style effective theory of finite-width nets; every analytic prediction validated against measured network statistics.
- **Telgarsky, deep learning theory notes** (https://mjt.cs.illinois.edu/dlt/). Approximation/optimization/generalization decomposition with deliberately *simplified, single-lecture-sized* proofs — a template for compressing rates elegantly.
- **Arora et al., *Theory of Deep Learning* draft** (https://www.cs.princeton.edu/~arora/TheoryDL.pdf). Still a course-notes draft (no 2025/26 refresh found); useful mainly as a roadmap/bibliography of non-convex-landscape and generalization literature.
- **Vershynin, *High-Dimensional Probability*** (free: https://www.math.uci.edu/~rvershyn/papers/HDP-book/HDP-2.pdf). Concentration, sub-Gaussian/sub-exponential, random matrices, JL — measure-theory-free, every inequality applied to a recognizable ML object.
- **Rigollet, MIT 18.657 *Mathematics of Machine Learning* notes** (arXiv:2310.19244) and **Nagler, *Statistical Learning Theory* (LMU 2024)** (https://tnagler.github.io/slt-2024.pdf) — the notes the Cambridge Part III and ETH curricula are literally assembled from ("Rigollet + Liang + Wainwright" is the current consensus SLT curriculum).

**Probability / information theory**

- **MacKay, *ITILA*** (free: https://www.inference.org.uk/mackay/itila/). Puzzle-first exposition of entropy, coding, and Bayesian inference with a large bank of solved exercises — the canonical model for making MLE/MAP/Bayes sticky.
- **Cover & Thomas, *Elements of Information Theory*** (2nd ed. 2006). Still the canonical axioms-through-rate-distortion reference every newer text positions itself against.
- **Polyanskiy & Wu, *Information Theory: From Coding to Learning*** (CUP 2025; free draft: https://people.lids.mit.edu/yp/homepage/data/itbook-export.pdf; MIT 6.7480). The modern successor: variational/convex-duality treatment of f-divergences, strong data-processing, PAC-Bayes, GAN losses, even LLM tokenization/quantization. The single best bridge from IT to 2026 DL.
- **Murphy, *Probabilistic Machine Learning: Advanced Topics*** (Dec 2025 draft: https://probml.github.io/pml-book/book2.html). Integrated MCMC + VI + generative-model treatment; the current encyclopedic reference for inference algorithms.

**Dynamics / generative models (fast-moving, 2024–2026)**

- **Särkkä & Solin, *Applied Stochastic Differential Equations*** (free: https://users.aalto.fi/~asolin/sde-book/sde-book.pdf; code: github.com/AaltoML/SDE). Brownian motion → Itô → Fokker–Planck → filtering, every concept simulatable — the model for an executable SDE chapter.
- **Kidger, *On Neural Differential Equations*** (arXiv:2202.02435). Complete pedagogical monograph on neural ODEs/CDEs/SDEs, organized by equation family with solver/adjoint chapters cleanly separated from modeling chapters.
- **Lipman et al. (Meta AI), *Flow Matching Guide and Code*** (arXiv:2412.06264; companion PyTorch library). Separates the *design space* (path, coupling, schedule) from the *algorithm*; covers discrete/Riemannian FM and guidance. The current FM reference.
- **MIT 6.S184/6.S975, *Flow Matching and Diffusion Models*** (Holderrieth & Erives; https://diffusion.csail.mit.edu/, 2026 edition live; notes: arXiv:2506.02070). Five lectures + three graduated labs ending in "build a latent diffusion model from scratch" — the single best notebook-ready Dynamics curriculum model.
- **Yang Song, score-based blog** (https://yang-song.net/blog/2021/score/). The SDE-unification framing (DDPM = VP-SDE, NCSN = VE-SDE, probability-flow ODE as deterministic counterpart) — the conceptual backbone a Dynamics chapter should adopt.
- **Lilian Weng, "What are Diffusion Models?"** (https://lilianweng.github.io/posts/2021-07-11-diffusion-models/, refreshed 2024). The clearest incremental build-up: forward process → ELBO → simplified loss → score matching → DDIM → guidance, each step motivated by a deficiency of the previous.
- **Karras et al., EDM** (arXiv:2206.00364). The design-space-table device: diffusion decomposed into orthogonal axes (schedule, preconditioning, parameterization, sampler).
- **Albergo, Boffi & Vanden-Eijnden, *Stochastic Interpolants*** (JMLR 2025, arXiv:2303.08797). Unifies flows and diffusions under one interpolant object with tunable diffusion; **Liu et al., Rectified Flow** (https://www.cs.utexas.edu/~lqiang/rectflow/html/intro.html) for the straight-path/reflow device.
- **CVPR 2026 tutorial "Analytic Understanding of Diffusion Models"** (https://analytic-diffusion.github.io/). Closed-form optimal-denoiser theory taught with *training-free* Jupyter notebooks — proof that meaningful diffusion pedagogy needs no GPU, directly relevant to this repo's CPU-only notebook constraint.

**New 2024–2026 competitor-scope monographs to watch**

- **Petersen & Zech, *Mathematical Theory of Deep Learning*** (arXiv:2407.18384, v4 Jan 2026) — approximation/optimization/generalization as three pillars; textbook-length.
- **Xiaojing Ye, *Mathematical Foundations of Deep Learning*** (arXiv:2603.18387, Mar 2026, Chapman & Hall/CRC, 308 pp) — approximation, training, optimal control, generative models, with probability/IT appendices.
- **Jentzen, Kuckuck & von Wurstemberger, *Mathematical Introduction to Deep Learning*** (arXiv:2310.20360) — rigorous universal approximation + SGD convergence.
- **Wang & Choromanska, "A Survey of Optimization Methods for Training DL Models"** (arXiv:2501.14458, Jan 2025) — most current single reference on Adam-family theory and practice.
- **Geshkovski, Letrouit, Polyanskiy & Rigollet, "A Mathematical Perspective on Transformers"** (Bull. AMS 2025, arXiv:2312.10794) — attention as an interacting particle system; plus **Elhage et al., "A Mathematical Framework for Transformer Circuits"** (https://transformer-circuits.pub/2021/framework/index.html).

**Course-syllabus finding.** NYU (Bruna MathsDL, last public edition 2020), Cambridge Part III (statslab "Mathematics of Machine Learning"; R252 is a paper-reading seminar), ETH (Bandeira notes), Princeton (Hanin/Arora) all *assume* LA/calculus/probability and start at learning theory. No elite course currently *teaches* the foundations with executable notebooks — the niche this appendix targets is genuinely underserved. Transformer math is emerging as its own subfield (DESY workshop Sept 2025; Oxford Mathematical Institute workshop Aug 2026).

### Pedagogical devices worth stealing

1. **Geometry before symbols (3Blue1Brown; Boyd's duality pictures).** Render the transform acting on a unit circle/grid *before* the algebraic definition, then immediately verify the geometric claim in code (plot image of unit circle under A, overlay scaled singular vectors). For duality: draw primal/dual feasible sets and watch the gap close live as a parameter varies — a notebook makes B&V's static figure interactive.
2. **One running example as a chapter spine (Strang's A=CR).** Carry a single data matrix through QR → eigen → SVD → low-rank truncation in successive cells, rather than presenting four unrelated decompositions.
3. **Every code cell verifies a claim just made in prose (VMLS; Bach's reproduce-every-figure ethos).** Not syntax demos: compute `cond(A)`, reconstruct from truncated SVD and print the error, race SGD vs. GD and plot against the proved rate. Bach ships code for essentially every figure — the strongest published precedent for d2l's "code teaches" rule.
4. **Puzzle-first sections with solved exercises (MacKay).** Open each probability/IT section with a concrete hand-computable puzzle (weighing problem, burglar alarm), solve it numerically in a cell, then generalize.
5. **Cost-motivated abstraction (Edelman & Johnson).** Motivate reverse-mode by counting FLOPs on a many-inputs-one-output Jacobian before naming it backprop; make it a `%timeit jax.jvp` vs `jax.vjp` cell. Similarly, Parr & Howard's three-way chain-rule taxonomy maps onto a tiny hand-built computational graph cross-checked against `torch.autograd`.
6. **Design-space tables with a toggleable implementation (Karras EDM; Lipman FM Guide).** One parameterized `Sampler`/`Schedule` class where each table row is a config; readers flip one axis at a time. Song's SDE unification is the same device at the conceptual level: one `sde_step()` with `type={VE,VP}` shows "different schedule, same machinery" empirically.
7. **Deficiency-gated incremental build-up (Lilian Weng).** Each step introduced by why the previous one isn't good enough: 1000-step ancestral → DDIM → exponential integrator, timing each swap.
8. **Theory validated against measurement in the same cell (Roberts & Yaida).** Plot measured preactivation statistics / gradient-noise of a small trained MLP against the analytic prediction side by side.
9. **Training-free notebooks for diffusion theory (CVPR 2026 Analytic Diffusion Studio; MIT 6.S184 labs).** Closed-form/KDE optimal denoisers demonstrate diffusion behavior with no GPU — directly compatible with this book's CPU-only committed-outputs constraint. MIT 6.S184's three graduated labs (2D toy score matching → full pipeline) are the best template for the Dynamics chapter's notebook arc.
10. **Executable proofs of backward formulas (differentiable-SVD literature).** Derive factorization gradients by implicit differentiation of the defining constraints, then confirm with `torch.autograd.gradcheck` — turning "here's the formula" into a runnable verification.
11. **Compressed single-lecture proofs (Telgarsky).** Trim each convergence proof to the minimum argument that still yields the rate — matches this project's "short, elegant, intuition-first proofs" convention.
12. **One variational formula, many instances (Polyanskiy & Wu).** Compute several f-divergence variational lower bounds on the same toy distribution pair in one cell and compare tightness — GAN objectives fall out as rows of a table.

**Bottom-line calibration.** The competitive field splits into (a) foundations texts that are strong but not notebook-native (MML, GBC, Strang), (b) code-forward texts that stop short of DL (VMLS, Bach), and (c) superb but fragmented 2024–2026 material on dynamics/generative math (Song, Weng, Lipman, MIT 6.S184) and matrix calculus (Edelman & Johnson, Blondel & Roulet). Nobody currently combines executable notebooks + foundations + 2026 generative/LLM-relevant math in one place; the clearest content gaps to own are numerical stability & LR-schedule math (Ch.24), eval statistics (Ch.25), tensor/einsum literacy (Ch.22), and — beyond the six buckets — transformer math, μP/scaling laws, generalization-beyond-VC, OT, and MCMC/VI.
