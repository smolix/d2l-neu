# Review — chapter_linear-classification/image-classification-dataset.md  (§4.2 "The Image Classification Dataset")

**Role in the chapter:** Introduces Fashion-MNIST — its provenance, split sizes, tensor layout, and the `FashionMNIST` `DataModule` class (load, iterate, visualize) — that every subsequent §4 file reuses. Pure infrastructure and dataset-orientation; no modelling.

**Verdict:** Functional and complete as a utility section, but two issues hold it below the best-textbook bar. First, the introductory paragraph buries the key pedagogical point (why Fashion-MNIST rather than MNIST) in filler prose about 1990s Sun SPARCStations that teaches nothing and will age poorly. Second, cell `image-classification-dataset-loading-the-dataset-3` (`data.train[0][0].shape`) produces *different* outputs across frameworks without any explanation — `torch.Size([1, 32, 32])` vs `(28, 28)` for TF/JAX — because the TF/JAX backend stores raw (H, W) tuples before the `get_dataloader` resize step; this silent divergence will confuse readers. Both fixes are surgical. The timing cell is borderline but defensible; the `show_images` stub is the right call (no inline drawing code). MXNet stderr walls in the committed outputs are a presentation issue.

**Grade:** B — assignable as infrastructure reading, but the intro prose is padded and the cross-framework shape inconsistency is a correctness-adjacent teaching bug.

## 1. Coverage

### Add

**Why Fashion-MNIST over MNIST (one sentence, in-place):** The intro circles around this but never states it crisply. The key facts are: (a) Fashion-MNIST has the same tensor shape and API as MNIST so all downstream code is identical, (b) it is harder — even simple linear models do not trivially saturate it — making accuracy differences between models meaningful. A one-sentence direct statement of (a) and (b) replaces ~150 words of historical filler.

**Tensor layout convention (minor gap):** The prose mentions `c × h × w` for PyTorch/MXNet but does not acknowledge the `h × w × c` convention used by TF/JAX (NHWC). Since both layouts appear in the executed outputs, a single parenthetical noting the channel-last vs. channel-first split — and that `get_dataloader` handles the difference transparently — would close this gap without expanding the section.

### Remove / trim

**1990s filler in the intro (~120 words):** The Sun SPARCStation 5, AT&T Bell Labs anecdote, active-set methods digression, and ImageNet-is-too-large apology are not teaching material. They were charming in the 2020 edition but now read as dated padding. Cut to: a tight motivation for Fashion-MNIST's role, then straight to loading.

**"sanity check" framing for MNIST:** The sentence "Today, MNIST serves as more of a sanity check than as a benchmark" is fine but is immediately followed by the active-set/boundary-seeking tangent that should be cut entirely.

**Summary paragraph (last two sentences):** "Lastly, data iterators are a key component…" repeats verbatim what the timing-cell prose already says. Trim to one crisper sentence or cut.

### Reorder / restructure

No structural reorder needed. The four-section spine (Loading → Minibatch → Visualization → Summary) is the right shape for a utility section. The intro block before the first `##` heading is where the trimming should happen.

## 2. Teaching quality

### Structure & flow

Good for a utility section: four flat `##` sections, no nesting needed at this scale. The section earns its place as a setup chapter. No restructuring required.

### Figures

No schematic figures exist or are needed — this is a data-loading section. The visualization output (eight labeled Fashion-MNIST samples) is the only figure and serves its purpose perfectly: readers see what they are working with. It is generated by the `visualize` method (not inline drawing code), which is the correct pattern.

One minor note: the figure has no `:label:` or `:numref:` anchor. Since it is an inline output cell rather than a pre-generated SVG, a `:numref:` is not required by convention, but downstream sections that want to reference "the Fashion-MNIST examples shown in §4.2" have no anchor to point to. This is a very low priority.

### Prose & clarity

**Worst offender (intro paragraph, lines ~8–18):** The Sun SPARCStation paragraph is ~170 words of historical color that do not help a reader understand Fashion-MNIST or the data-loading API. The LeNet-5 / SVM / tangent-distance citations, while accurate, belong in the CNN chapter's history section, not here. Cut to ~60 words.

**"We only need to know how to invoke `d2l.show_images` rather than how it works"** (Visualization section) — this is a reasonable pedagogical disclaimer but is undercut by the `raise NotImplementedError` stub: readers who look at the stub will think the function is broken. A brief comment (`# full implementation in d2l library`) would resolve the confusion.

**Shape cell prose:** "The images are grayscale and upscaled to 32×32 pixels in resolution above" — "above" is vague; the resize happens inside the `FashionMNIST` constructor with `resize=(32, 32)`. Clearer: "We requested `resize=(32, 32)` above, so each example is a 1×32×32 tensor (single grayscale channel, height, width)."

**TF/JAX shape inconsistency:** Cell `loading-the-dataset-3` is untagged (applies to all frameworks) but calls `data.train[0][0].shape`. For PyTorch/MXNet this returns the post-resize tensor shape because `transform_first` is applied at load time. For TF/JAX, `data.train` holds raw numpy arrays from `keras.datasets.fashion_mnist.load_data()` — shape `(60000, 28, 28)` — so `data.train[0]` is the 60000-image array and `data.train[0][0]` is a single `(28, 28)` image (not yet resized, no channel dim). The reader sees `torch.Size([1, 32, 32])` vs `(28, 28)` across tabs with no explanation, which looks like a bug. **This is a P1 teaching bug.**

### Exercises

The three exercises are shallow: Q1 is a 30-second experiment, Q2 is open-ended without direction, Q3 ("which other datasets are available?") is a documentation lookup. For a top program, at minimum one exercise should require the student to reason or compute.

Proposed replacements (see ICD-5 in §4):
1. *(retain, sharpen)* What happens to throughput as `batch_size` → 1? As `batch_size` → full dataset? Plot throughput vs. `batch_size` and explain the shape of the curve.
2. *(new)* Set `num_workers=0` and time one epoch; then `num_workers=4`. Explain the difference. Under what conditions does increasing `num_workers` stop helping?
3. *(new)* PyTorch stores images as `(C, H, W)` ("channel-first"), while TensorFlow uses `(H, W, C)` ("channel-last"). What does the `get_dataloader` method do to bridge this? Modify the TF/JAX loader to output channel-first tensors and verify the shape.

## 3. Code & examples

### Does the code teach?

Mostly yes. The `FashionMNIST` class, `get_dataloader`, `text_labels`, and `visualize` cells all teach the DataModule pattern. The `show_images` stub is a defensible choice (keeps the section from becoming a matplotlib wall). The timing cell is borderline — see ICD-4.

### PyTorch

Clean and idiomatic for PyTorch 2.x. `torchvision.datasets.FashionMNIST` + `torch.utils.data.DataLoader` is the canonical pattern. `transforms.Compose([transforms.Resize(resize), transforms.ToTensor()])` is correct. No issues.

### JAX

The JAX loader correctly uses TF data for I/O (`tfds.as_numpy(tf.data.Dataset...)`) — this is the recommended pattern. The import cell includes `import time` appropriately. The `drop_remainder=train` comment is well-explained. Minor: `import numpy as np` is imported but `np` is not used anywhere in the visible cells (it may be used by `d2l` internals). Low priority.

The `(28, 28)` shape output from `loading-the-dataset-3` is the main issue — see ICD-1.

### TensorFlow

`tf.keras.datasets.fashion_mnist.load_data()` is current Keras 3 API. The `get_dataloader` pipeline (`expand_dims → /255 → shuffle → batch → resize_with_pad`) is correct and well-commented. `drop_remainder=train` is correct.

Same `(28, 28)` shape bug as JAX. Additionally the prose says "upscaled to 32×32 pixels" but the raw TF/JAX data is still 28×28 at the point `loading-the-dataset-3` probes it — this is the root of the inconsistency.

### MXNet

**Currency issue:** Apache MXNet was archived by the ASF in September 2023. The MXNet tab uses `gluon.data.vision.FashionMNIST` which is functional in the custom 2.0 wheel maintained in this repo, so the code is not broken. However, presenting MXNet as a co-equal live framework is misleading in 2026. This is a book-wide decision (flagged here for the cross-file overview); no per-file code changes are needed beyond what the overview decides.

**MXNet stderr walls:** Cells `reading-a-minibatch-2`, `reading-a-minibatch-3`, and `visualization-2` all emit multi-kilobyte oneDNN storage-type fallback warnings in the committed output manifests. These render verbatim on the HTML page and are pedagogically inert noise. Setting `MXNET_STORAGE_FALLBACK_LOG_VERBOSE=0` in the MXNet `get_dataloader` cell (or the imports cell) would suppress them. See ICD-3.

### Cross-framework consistency & d2l conventions

**Single divergence that matters:** The untagged `loading-the-dataset-3` cell silently produces different shapes across frameworks because the frameworks store their raw data differently before the `get_dataloader` transform step. All other cells are properly tagged per-framework.

**Imports cell:** All four frameworks have a single imports cell (`image-classification-dataset-the-image-classification-dataset`). No re-imports. `#@save` marks are correctly placed on `FashionMNIST`, `text_labels`, `get_dataloader`, `visualize`. `show_images` is `#@save` as a stub — that is correct; the real implementation is in the d2l library.

**One imports issue (JAX):** `import numpy as np` is present in the JAX imports cell but `np` does not appear in any JAX-tagged cell in this file. This is a minor smell (either it is used by a `d2l` method called here, or it is a leftover import).

  ---
  One widely used benchmark for image classification is [MNIST](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MNIST_database) :cite:`LeCun.Bottou.Bengio.ea.1998`, a dataset of 70,000 handwritten-digit images ($28 \times 28$ pixels, 10 classes). MNIST shaped a generation of machine learning research, but today even simple linear models exceed 95% accuracy — differences between strong and weak models are hard to see. We need a dataset where a linear baseline is clearly outpaced by a richer model.

  We therefore use **Fashion-MNIST** :cite:`Xiao.Rasul.Vollgraf.2017`, a drop-in replacement released in 2017. It has exactly the same structure — 60,000 training and 10,000 test images of $28 \times 28$ grayscale pixels, 10 classes — but the classes are clothing categories (T-shirt, trouser, pullover, …) that are harder to separate, making accuracy differences between models clearly visible. For large-scale experiments the standard benchmark is ImageNet :cite:`Deng.Dong.Socher.ea.2009` (1.2 M images, 1000 classes), but it is too large for interactive illustration here; Fashion-MNIST provides the same qualitative lessons at a fraction of the compute cost.

## 5. Keep — what is already excellent (do not lose this)

- **`DataModule` / `#@save` pattern.** The `FashionMNIST` class cleanly subclasses `d2l.DataModule`, stores hyperparameters via `save_hyperparameters()`, and delegates to per-framework loaders. This is exactly the right abstraction for reuse across §4 and beyond.
- **`show_images` as a stub.** Declaring the interface and deferring the matplotlib internals to the library is the correct application of the house rule ("code that only draws does not belong in the teaching notebook"). Do not import the implementation.
- **`visualize` method.** Binding visualization to the dataset class (not a free function) is elegant and reusable; the output (eight labeled garment images) is the right sanity check.
- **TF/JAX `get_dataloader` with `drop_remainder=train` and its comment.** The inline explanation of why `drop_remainder` prevents retracing is a genuine teaching point about tf.data and JIT compilation. Preserve it.
- **`text_labels` helper.** Concise, stored as `#@save`, immediately makes outputs human-readable. The list of ten class names is exactly right.
- **Timing cell placement.** Timing the full epoch immediately after showing the dataloader is the right place — it grounds the "fast enough" claim before moving on to visualization. Keep the cell; only strengthen the surrounding prose (ICD-4).
- **No framework-divergent business logic.** All four frameworks produce the same training/val splits (60 k / 10 k), the same class labels, and the same external interface. The divergence is correctly confined to I/O mechanics (`gluon.data` vs `torchvision` vs `tf.data` + tfds).
